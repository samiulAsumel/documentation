# PHASE 1 — The Port as a Business System

## 1.1 What Is a Port?

A port is a **logistics interface** between sea and land. It is not just a place where ships stop — it is a revenue-generating infrastructure system where every square meter, every crane move, and every container day translates into money.

A port earns money by:
- Charging vessels for using its berths, tugs, pilots, and facilities
- Charging cargo owners for handling, storage, and gate services
- Providing value-added services (reefer, customs, stuffing/stripping)

A port loses money through:
- Idle berths (no vessel = no revenue)
- Idle equipment (cranes not moving = wasted capital)
- Excess dwell time (congested yard = blocked capacity)
- Poor planning (delays, rehandles, missed vessel windows)

**Key principle:** A port manager's job is to maximize throughput while minimizing cost, delay, and risk.

# PHASE 2 — Vessel Operations (Deep Theory)

## 2.1 Vessel Arrival Process — Step by Step

Every vessel call follows a sequence. As a manager, you must know every step and who is responsible.

**Pre-arrival (72 hours before ETA):**
1. Shipping line submits pre-arrival notification (ETA, vessel details, cargo manifest)
2. Ship agent submits port entry documentation to port authority
3. Dangerous goods (DG) pre-declaration submitted to terminal
4. Berth pre-allocation confirmed by port planning team
5. Customs pre-clearance initiated for priority cargo

**Arrival (12–24 hours before berthing):**
1. Vessel enters pilot boarding ground (outer anchorage)
2. Pilot boards vessel at pilot station
3. Tug escort begins for large/high-windage vessels
4. Vessel proceeds to berth under pilot guidance
5. Mooring lines secured — "All-Fast" declared
6. Gangway rigged — shore access established
7. Port Health, Immigration, Customs board for port clearance
8. Terminal receives confirmed bay plan (BAPLIE file) from vessel
9. Cargo operations commence

**Operations:**
1. Stevedoring team assigned to vessel
2. STS cranes positioned to working bays
3. Discharge operations begin (inbound containers off vessel)
4. Load operations follow discharge sequence
5. Ship's officer signs Mate's Receipt for loaded cargo
6. Tally clerks verify move-by-move against manifest

**Departure:**
1. Final tally — confirmed loaded vs planned
2. Bill of Lading issued / Mate's Receipt converted
3. Customs out-clearance
4. Pilot reboarded
5. Tugs assist departure
6. Port log closed — berth released

## 2.2 Berth Planning — The Manager's Core Skill

**Berth allocation is one of the highest-impact decisions in port management.** A wrong berth assignment causes congestion, vessel delays, demurrage claims, and revenue loss.

### Key Factors in Berth Allocation:

**Physical constraints:**
- Berth length — vessel LOA (Length Overall) must fit with safety margin (minimum 10% clearance)
- Water depth (chart datum) — vessel's maximum draft must not exceed available depth
- Tidal window — if channel/berth depth is tide-dependent, vessel can only enter/depart in specific tidal hours
- Air draft — vessel height must clear any overhead structures (bridges, power lines)
- Fender capacity — berth fenders must handle the vessel's displacement

**Operational priorities:**
- Liner service vessels have fixed windows — they must be accommodated first
- Feeder vessels are more flexible but fill fast
- First-come-first-served (FCFS) is the baseline, but priority rules override it
- Vessel turnaround time targets influence berth sequencing
- Cargo volume at berth determines crane allocation

**Planning conflict scenarios a manager must handle:**
- Two vessels requesting same berth simultaneously → priority matrix decision
- Vessel arrives early (early bird) → berth not ready → anchorage waiting
- Vessel arrives late → downstream vessel must wait or shift berths
- Vessel requires emergency extension → cascading delay management

### Berth Productivity Formula:
```
Berth Productivity = Total Moves ÷ Total Berth Hours (All-Fast to Lines-Away)
```

World-class target: **25+ moves per hour** per berth (for container terminals)  
Saudi terminal targets: **28–32 gross moves per hour**

## 2.4 Notice of Readiness (NOR) and Its Billing Significance

**NOR (Notice of Readiness)** is the formal notice tendered by the ship's master to the charterer or agent, declaring that the vessel has arrived at the port and is ready to load or discharge cargo.

Why NOR matters for a manager:
- NOR triggers the **laytime clock** — the time allowed for cargo operations under the charter party
- If operations exceed laytime → **demurrage** starts (vessel charges the port/charterer per day)
- If operations finish early → **despatch** may be earned (charterer receives a credit)
- Incorrect NOR timing leads to costly billing disputes

**NOR tender sequence:**
1. Vessel arrives at pilot boarding ground or anchorage
2. Master tenders NOR to agent (via email or formal letter)
3. Agent acknowledges with timestamp
4. Laytime begins per charter party terms (immediately, or after berth is available)
5. All-Fast at berth — operations begin
6. Time lost due to port delays is "on demurrage" (port liability)
7. Time lost due to vessel issues is "off demurrage" (vessel liability)

# PHASE 3 — Yard Management (Advanced Theory)

## 3.1 The Yard as a System

The container yard is the port's most critical operational area. It is both a storage facility and a logistics processing center. Poor yard management is the number one cause of:
- Vessel delays (can't find or retrieve containers for loading)
- High rehandle rates (wasted crane moves)
- Revenue loss (yard congestion reduces capacity)
- Customer dissatisfaction (cargo hard to access)

**A container yard manager controls:**
- Where every container is physically located (position = bay-row-tier)
- How long each container stays (dwell time)
- When containers are moved to prepare for vessel loading (pre-marshalling)
- Which equipment is assigned to each stack block

---

## 3.2 Container Position Address System

Every container in the yard has a precise three-dimensional address:

```
BAY — ROW — TIER
04  —  C  —  2
```

- **Bay:** Numbered longitudinal position in the block (e.g., Bay 01, 03, 05...)
- **Row:** Lettered position from quay to inland side (A, B, C, D...)
- **Tier:** Height from ground (Tier 1 = ground, Tier 5 = top)

The TOS system tracks every container by this address at all times. As a manager, you read yard plans and position reports to identify:
- Congested blocks (over 80% utilized)
- Containers buried under wrong cargo (rehandle risk)
- Reefer zones at capacity
- Hazmat containers requiring segregation checks

---

## 3.3 Stack Planning Logic

**The fundamental challenge of stack planning:** Containers are stacked vertically, but they leave in a specific sequence. If the container you need is at the bottom of a stack, you must move all containers on top of it first — these are **rehandles**.

**Principles of good stack planning:**

1. **Group by vessel/load sequence:** All containers for the same vessel, same bay, same load sequence should be in the same stack area. This minimizes retrieval moves.

2. **Group by discharge sequence:** Import containers should be organized by consignee or C&F agent zone so trucks can access them without crane interference.

3. **Weight segregation:** Heavy containers at the bottom of stacks, lighter ones on top. This is both a safety rule and a crane productivity rule.

4. **Dwell time zoning:** Short-dwell transshipment containers near quay (fast turnover). Long-dwell import containers at back of yard.

5. **Pre-marshalling:** Before a vessel arrives, re-arrange containers in the yard into the correct load sequence. This is done 12–24 hours in advance. Pre-marshalling moves are non-productive (they don't load or discharge cargo) but they prevent worse delays during vessel operations.

**Rehandle ratio formula:**
```
Rehandle Ratio (%) = (Rehandle Moves ÷ Total Moves) × 100
```

World-class target: **below 8%**  
Poor performance: **above 20%** (indicates serious planning failure)

## 3.5 Equipment in the Yard

**RTG (Rubber Tyred Gantry) Crane:**
- Straddles a row of containers in a stack block
- Moves between blocks by driving on rubber tyres (can relocate)
- Lifts containers up to 5 high (some 6 high)
- Productivity: 15–20 moves per hour
- Requires operator + ground staff for safe operation
- Modern RTGs are semi-automated or remote-operated

**RMG (Rail Mounted Gantry) Crane:**
- Fixed to rails — cannot move between blocks
- Higher productivity than RTG: 20–25 moves per hour
- Used in automated terminals (King Abdullah Port uses ARMGs)
- Lower operating cost in the long run, higher capital cost

**Reach Stacker:**
- Flexible, fast-moving yard equipment
- Can reach over 3–4 rows (unlike forklifts)
- Used in smaller terminals or for OOG/special cargo
- Less productive than RTG but much more flexible

**Terminal Tractor + Chassis:**
- Moves containers between quay and yard
- Cannot stack — only horizontal transport
- Number of tractors must match crane productivity to avoid quay congestion

# PHASE 4 — Cargo Flow and Transshipment

## 4.1 The Five Cargo Flow Types

Every container that passes through a terminal follows one of five distinct flows. Each flow has different operational requirements, billing rules, and KPIs.

### Flow 1: Import
```
Ocean Vessel → Discharge → Yard (dwell) → Gate Out → Consignee
```
- Highest dwell time (awaiting Customs clearance and consignee collection)
- Generates most storage revenue
- Dwell time reduction is a key port-authority priority (ties up yard capacity)
- Free time: typically 3–5 calendar days
- After free time: tiered slab storage charges

### Flow 2: Export
```
Shipper → Gate In → Yard (pre-stack) → Load → Ocean Vessel
```
- Container received 3–7 days before vessel arrival
- Port must verify VGM (Verified Gross Mass) before stacking
- Customs export clearance required before stacking in some countries
- Arranged in yard by vessel bay sequence before vessel arrives
- Free time: typically 5–7 days before vessel ETA

### Flow 3: Transshipment (Same Wharf / Direct)
```
Vessel A → Discharge → Direct Transfer (no yard) → Load → Vessel B
```
- Fastest and cheapest transshipment option
- Requires both vessels at same wharf simultaneously
- No storage cost, minimal handling
- Logistically complex (requires synchronized berthing plan)
- Common at hub ports for high-volume feeder connections

### Flow 4: Transshipment (Different Wharf / Via Yard)
```
Vessel A → Discharge → Yard (transit zone) → Yard → Load → Vessel B
```
- Most common transshipment type at large hubs
- Container enters transit zone — typically 14–21 days free time
- Longer free time reflects the operational reality of feeder scheduling
- Generates transport charge (inter-terminal movement) + handling (x2)
- KPI: Transshipment dwell time — target <7 days in transit yard

### Flow 5: Coastal / Feeder
```
Main Port → Discharge → Load → Coastal Vessel → Regional Port
```
- Lower tariff rate than deep-sea (competitive pricing)
- Shorter vessel calls, smaller vessels
- Critical for Saudi inland distribution to smaller Gulf ports

## 4.2 Transshipment Hub Operations — Saudi Context

Saudi Arabia's position on the Red Sea and Arabian Gulf makes it a strategic transshipment hub. **Jeddah Islamic Port** and **King Abdullah Port** serve as the primary Red Sea transshipment hubs for East-West and North-South trade lanes.

**Why transshipment matters at Saudi ports:**
- 40–60% of container volume at Jeddah is transshipment
- Mainline ULCV vessels cannot call at every small Red Sea port
- Hub-and-spoke model: ULCV calls at Jeddah → feeder vessels distribute to Aqaba, Djibouti, Hodeidah, etc.
- Speed of transshipment (dwell time in transit) is the competitive differentiator between hub ports

**The manager's role in transshipment:**
- Coordinate vessel windows for mainline and feeder arrivals
- Minimize transit dwell in yard
- Pre-plan load sequence before feeder vessel arrives
- Manage crane allocation between discharging mainline and loading feeder simultaneously
- Resolve missing connection cargo (cargo that misses the connecting feeder)

# PHASE 5 — Terminal Operating System (TOS)

## 5.1 What Is a TOS and Why Must a Manager Understand It?

The **Terminal Operating System (TOS)** is the core software platform that manages all terminal operations. It is the single source of truth for:
- Where every container is located
- What every crane and piece of equipment is doing
- What every vessel plan looks like
- What every billing charge has been generated

A port operations manager does not need to be a programmer — but must understand the TOS well enough to:
- Read real-time operational dashboards
- Understand why a billing event was or was not triggered
- Investigate complaints from shipping lines or C&F agents
- Identify operational bottlenecks from system data
- Communicate requirements to the IT/TOS team

**Major TOS systems used at global ports:**
- **NAVIS N4 (Cargotec)** — dominant global TOS, used at most major terminals including Saudi ports
- **SPARCS N4** — NAVIS's operational planning module
- **Tideworks** — strong in North America
- **COSMOS** — DP World's proprietary TOS
- **Saab Portware** — used in Scandinavian and some Middle Eastern ports
- **CargoWise** — broader logistics + port integration

---

## 5.2 Core TOS Modules

### Vessel Planning Module
- Holds the vessel schedule (ETA, ETD, berth assignment)
- Receives the BAPLIE (bay plan) from the shipping line
- Allows planners to build the load plan (which container goes to which bay)
- Generates the crane work instruction list
- Tracks real-time progress: discharged, loaded, remaining

### Yard Management Module
- Maintains real-time inventory of every container in the yard
- Assigns yard positions based on rules (zone, vessel, weight, dwell)
- Generates RTG/crane job instructions
- Tracks dwell time from day of entry
- Flags containers that have exceeded free time for billing trigger
- Manages pre-marshalling job lists

### Gate Operations Module
- Pre-advice processing (C&F agent submits container details before truck arrives)
- OCR (Optical Character Recognition) reads container number from truck at gate
- Confirms customs release status before gate-out authorized
- Generates gate-in and gate-out timestamps (critical for billing)
- Tracks driver ID, truck plate, seal number

### Equipment Control System (ECS)
- Job dispatch to crane operators (RTG, STS)
- Real-time tracking of equipment location (RTLS — Real-Time Location System)
- Tracks idle time vs productive time
- Interfaces with crane automation systems
- Records moves per shift per crane (productivity input)

### Billing Integration Module
- Receives events from all modules (discharge, gate-in, gate-out, load)
- Applies tariff rules automatically (free time, slab rates, charge codes)
- Generates pro-forma and final invoices
- Manages debit notes, credit notes
- Exports to financial ERP system (SAP, Oracle)

## 6.2 Free Time and Slab Rate Logic

**Free time** is the grace period after cargo arrives in the terminal during which no storage charge is levied. It encourages prompt cargo collection and is a competitive tool ports use to attract cargo.

### Free Time Rules:

- Free time begins from the **day of discharge** (for imports) or **day of gate-in** (for exports)
- It is measured in **calendar days** or **working days** — the tariff specifies which
- Free time for transshipment is longer because feeder vessel connections are not always immediate

### Tiered Slab Rate Example (Import):

| Period | Rate |
|--------|------|
| Day 1–4 (free time) | No charge |
| Day 5–7 (slab 1) | USD 5 per TEU per day |
| Day 8–14 (slab 2) | USD 12 per TEU per day |
| Day 15+ (slab 3) | USD 25 per TEU per day |

**Key calculation rules:**
- The day of discharge counts as Day 1 in most tariffs
- If cargo leaves on Day 4 (within free time) → zero storage charge
- If cargo leaves on Day 10 → charges apply for Days 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 (slabs 1 and 2 apply)
- Maximum charge caps exist in some tariffs (protects cargo owners from runaway charges)

### Why free time management matters for a manager:
- Long average dwell time = yard congestion = reduced capacity = less revenue from new cargo
- Incentivizing faster collection requires understanding free time structure
- Reporting average dwell by cargo type, shipping line, and month is a standard manager task

## 6.3 Demurrage, Detention, and Storage — The Critical Distinction

These three terms are frequently confused. A manager must explain them precisely.

**Storage (Port charge):**
- Charged by the **terminal / port authority**
- Applies when a container stays in the **terminal yard** beyond free time
- Charged per container per day
- Covered in the port tariff

**Demurrage (Shipping line charge on the container):**
- Charged by the **shipping line**
- Applies when the importer/consignee keeps the container beyond the shipping line's free time
- Container has been **collected from port** but not returned empty
- Covered in the shipping line's tariff (separate from port tariff)
- Common confusion: "demurrage" is sometimes used loosely to mean port storage — technically incorrect

**Vessel Demurrage (Charter party charge):**
- Charged by the **vessel owner / charterer**
- Applies when the vessel stays at berth longer than allowed laytime
- This is a shipping/charter level charge, not a port-to-cargo charge
- Port authority may face this claim if port-side delays are responsible

**Detention (Shipping line charge on the container):**
- Charged by the **shipping line**
- Applies when the container/equipment is used for too long for inland transport
- Distinct from demurrage because it covers the container being out of port entirely

## 6.4 Invoice Structure and Dispute Resolution

As a manager, you will review billing disputes daily. Understanding invoice structure is essential.

**Invoice elements a manager must verify:**
1. Container number and ISO type code (20GP, 40GP, 40HQ, 45G1, etc.)
2. Date of discharge / date of gate-in (free time start)
3. Date of gate-out / date of loading (charge end)
4. Number of storage days calculated
5. Slab bracket applied
6. Tariff code referenced
7. Weight/OOG surcharge if applicable
8. Reefer days if applicable
9. VAT / levy applied correctly
10. Currency and exchange rate applied

**Common billing disputes:**
- Incorrect free time start date (disputed discharge date)
- Container held by Customs — port often grants an extension in these cases
- Equipment malfunction causing container not delivered on time
- Double charging (container charged under two accounts)
- Wrong container type rate applied

**Dispute resolution process:**
1. C&F agent raises dispute with formal letter + supporting documents
2. Billing team reviews TOS event log (timestamps are the source of truth)
3. If port error → credit note issued
4. If agent error → provide explanation and reject dispute
5. If Customs delay → apply force majeure / exception policy
6. All disputes must be resolved within defined SLA (typically 5–10 working days)

# PHASE 7 — Port KPIs and Performance Management

## 7.1 The KPI Hierarchy

KPIs in port management operate at three levels:

**Operational level (shift supervisor / floor manager):**
Tracked hourly or per shift. Immediate action if targets missed.
- Crane moves per hour (net and gross)
- Trucks served per hour at gate
- Real-time yard utilization %
- Equipment downtime events

**Tactical level (operations manager / senior manager):**
Tracked daily and weekly. Drives planning adjustments.
- Berth productivity per vessel call
- Average dwell time by cargo category
- Rehandle ratio per week
- Vessel waiting time
- Gate queue length and turnaround time

**Strategic level (terminal director / CEO):**
Tracked monthly and annually. Drives investment and commercial decisions.
- Annual throughput (TEU)
- Revenue per TEU
- Market share vs competing terminals
- Customer satisfaction score (shipping line feedback)
- Safety incident rate

---

## 7.2 Master KPI Reference Table

| KPI | Formula | Good | Warning | Critical |
|-----|---------|------|---------|----------|
| Berth productivity | Total moves ÷ berth hours | >25/hr | 18–25/hr | <18/hr |
| Net crane rate | Moves ÷ crane working hours | >30/hr | 22–30/hr | <22/hr |
| Vessel waiting time | Anchorage time before berthing | <4 hrs | 4–12 hrs | >12 hrs |
| Vessel TAT | Lines-away minus All-Fast | Per schedule | +10% | +25%+ |
| Yard utilization | Occupied TEUs ÷ capacity × 100 | 65–80% | 80–90% | >90% |
| Import dwell time | Gate-out date minus discharge date | <4 days | 4–8 days | >8 days |
| Export dwell time | Vessel load date minus gate-in date | <5 days | 5–10 days | >10 days |
| Transshipment dwell | Load date minus discharge date | <7 days | 7–14 days | >14 days |
| Rehandle ratio | Rehandles ÷ total moves × 100 | <8% | 8–15% | >15% |
| Gate truck TAT | Gate-out time minus gate-in time | <30 min | 30–60 min | >60 min |
| Equipment utilization | Active hours ÷ available hours × 100 | >75% | 60–75% | <60% |
| Reefer plug utilization | Occupied plugs ÷ available plugs × 100 | 70–85% | >90% | Capacity crisis |
| Revenue per TEU | Total revenue ÷ TEUs handled | Maximize | Track trend | Significant decline |

## 7.3 Root Cause Analysis — How a Manager Uses KPIs

A manager does not just report KPIs — a manager uses them to find and fix problems.

**Example scenario:** Berth productivity drops from 28 to 19 moves/hour for 3 consecutive vessel calls.

**Step-by-step investigation:**
1. Pull crane productivity logs from TOS — identify which crane(s) are underperforming
2. Check equipment downtime log — was there a breakdown?
3. Check yard crane (RTG) availability — were yard cranes keeping up with quay cranes?
4. Check vessel bay plan — were there complex operational bays (mixed weight, OOG, hazmat)?
5. Check weather log — wind speed above 20 knots restricts crane operations
6. Interview shift supervisor — any gang issues, lashing delays, documentation holds?
7. Check vessel cooperation — was ship crew slow in confirming holds clear?

**Cause identified → Action taken:**
- RTG bottleneck → increase RTG allocation to quay side
- Equipment failure → escalate to maintenance + review PM schedule
- Gang shortage → review shift manning levels
- Lashing delays → review lashing contractor performance

# PHASE 8 — Safety, Regulations, and Compliance

## 8.1 Core International Regulations

As a port operations manager, you are legally responsible for compliance with these frameworks:

**SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea):**
- Foundation international convention (IMO)
- Governs vessel construction, equipment, and cargo safety
- Chapter VI covers cargo handling and securing
- VGM (Verified Gross Mass) amendment is under SOLAS Chapter VI
- Violation = vessel can be detained by Port State Control

**MARPOL (Marine Pollution Prevention):**
- Controls vessel pollution: oil, chemicals, sewage, garbage, air emissions
- Port must provide waste reception facilities for vessels
- Port manager must ensure compliance with garbage declaration on arrival
- Accidental spills in port → port authority incident response required

**ISPS Code (International Ship and Port Facility Security Code):**
- Mandatory since 2004 following 9/11
- Every port facility must have an approved **Port Facility Security Plan (PFSP)**
- A designated **Port Facility Security Officer (PFSO)** is required
- Three security levels:
  - Level 1 (Normal): Standard screening, access control, CCTV
  - Level 2 (Heightened): Enhanced checks, restricted access zones, increased surveillance
  - Level 3 (Exceptional threat): Possible port closure, evacuation procedures, full lockdown
- Vessel and port must agree on a **Declaration of Security (DoS)** before operations
- Saudi ports are all ISPS-certified — foreign vessels without valid ISSC (ship security certificate) may be denied entry

**IMDG Code (International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code):**
- Classifies hazardous materials into 9 IMO classes
- Class 1 (Explosives) through Class 9 (Miscellaneous)
- Port manager must ensure proper segregation, stowage, and emergency planning
- Hazmat incident in yard → emergency response plan activated immediately

**VGM — Verified Gross Mass (SOLAS Amendment 2016):**
- Shipper is legally responsible for providing accurate gross mass of packed container
- Two methods: Method 1 (weigh the packed container); Method 2 (sum of cargo weights + tare)
- Terminal cannot load a container without a VGM declaration
- Overweight containers are a major cause of vessel instability incidents

## 8.2 Saudi Regulatory Framework

**Mawani — Saudi Ports Authority:**
- Government body overseeing all 9 commercial Saudi seaports
- Issues port licenses, approves tariffs, sets operational standards
- Vision 2030 target: increase port capacity and efficiency, attract foreign terminal operators
- Key Mawani KPI targets (Vision 2030): double throughput at major ports, reduce vessel TAT by 50%

**ZATCA — Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority:**
- Saudi equivalent of Customs authority
- Integrated with port operations for Customs clearance
- Cargo cannot leave port without ZATCA clearance (gate-out blocked in TOS)
- Saudi-specific: halal certification required for food cargo
- Strategic commodities (oil and gas equipment) have expedited clearance channels

**Saudi Coastguard:**
- Joint patrol with port security for waterside
- Vessel boarding for safety/security checks
- Emergency response coordination

## 8.3 Port Safety Management

**Risk areas a manager must actively manage:**

1. **Quayside safety:** Falls into water, crane overreach, lashing accidents. Mandatory PPE zones, banksman protocols.

2. **Yard safety:** RTG/reach stacker pedestrian collision risk. Strict pedestrian exclusion zones. Speed limits for terminal tractors.

3. **Dangerous goods handling:** IMO class segregation, MSDS availability, emergency spill kits pre-positioned.

4. **Fire safety:** Reefer electrical fires, hazmat incidents. Terminal fire brigade on standby, water supply pre-planned.

5. **Gate safety:** Traffic management for truck queueing. Pedestrian-vehicle separation at gate complex.

6. **Lifting operations:** All crane operators certified. Pre-shift crane inspection checklist mandatory.

**Manager's safety responsibilities:**
- Maintain accident/incident log and submit monthly safety reports to port authority
- Conduct regular safety drills (fire, oil spill, hazmat, man overboard)
- Ensure all contractors working in terminal have valid safety induction certification
- Participate in ISPS audit (internal annually, external every 5 years)
- Immediately report notifiable incidents to Mawani and relevant authority

# PHASE 10 — Interview Mastery

## 10.1 Senior Operations Interview Questions and Model Answers

---

**Q: A vessel arrives 8 hours early. The planned berth is occupied. What do you do?**

*Model answer:* I would first confirm the vessel's arrival via the pilot station and ship agent, then assess the status of the vessel currently at berth — estimated time of completion and Lines-Away. I would check whether any adjacent berth or temporary anchorage can accommodate the early vessel. I would calculate the impact on downstream vessel schedule and notify all stakeholders: ship agent, stevedoring team, and berth planner. If the current vessel can be expedited through additional equipment allocation to clear the berth faster, I would authorize that. If not, the early vessel waits at anchorage, and I issue a formal waiting time record — which becomes relevant if any demurrage claim arises. I would document the full sequence with timestamps for the operations log.

---

**Q: Your yard utilization reaches 92%. What actions do you take?**

*Model answer:* At 92%, the yard is in a critical state — any further intake risks total congestion. Immediate actions: First, I would freeze acceptance of new empty containers and redirect them to off-dock empty depots. Second, I would identify containers that have exceeded 14 days dwell time (long-dwell imports) and issue urgent notices to C&F agents — escalating to port authority if needed to exercise lien on cargo. Third, I would increase storage charges for extended dwell (if tariff allows tiered escalation above a threshold). Fourth, I would check if any bonded containers can be relocated to a bonded inland facility to free yard space. Fifth, I would brief the commercial team — no new vessel acceptances unless we can quantify space availability. I would report the situation to management with a recovery plan and daily yard utilization updates until we are below 80%.

---

**Q: How do you reduce rehandle ratio from 18% to below 8%?**

*Model answer:* An 18% rehandle ratio indicates a systemic yard planning failure. I would address it in four areas. First, improve pre-arrival planning — load lists from shipping lines must be received and processed at least 24 hours before vessel arrival so the TOS can pre-assign correct yard positions by load sequence. Second, enforce block discipline — export containers for the same vessel must be concentrated in designated blocks, not scattered across the yard due to ad-hoc placement. Third, improve pre-marshalling — the night shift before a vessel arrives should complete pre-marshalling moves, so load sequence is ready before the crane starts. Fourth, investigate the TOS yard planning rules — if the algorithm is placing containers sub-optimally, the parameters need tuning. I would set a 30-day improvement target and track the ratio on a daily dashboard.

---

**Q: A C&F agent disputes a USD 12,000 storage invoice, claiming their container was held by Customs and they should not be charged. How do you handle it?**

*Model answer:* First, I would request the agent provide the official Customs hold documentation with timestamps. Then I would pull the TOS event log for that container — entry date, free time start, Customs hold flag (if raised in the system), and gate-out date. If the port's TOS shows a Customs hold was registered during the dwell period, our tariff's force majeure or Customs delay provision likely applies, and I would authorize a credit note for the days under Customs control. If no hold was registered, I would ask the agent why the hold was not notified to the port in writing at the time — our tariff typically requires the hold to be declared to the terminal to qualify for relief. If agent fault (no notification) → charge stands. If terminal fault (hold not recorded when notified) → credit note. All decisions are documented and approved by the billing manager. The SLA for resolving this is 5 working days.

---

**Q: How would you implement a KPI improvement program at a low-performing terminal?**

*Model answer:* I would structure it in three phases. Phase 1 — Diagnose (weeks 1–2): Pull 3 months of TOS data and calculate all major KPIs — crane productivity, dwell time, rehandle ratio, vessel waiting time, gate TAT. Benchmark against industry standards. Identify the top 3 pain points by revenue and operational impact. Phase 2 — Quick wins (weeks 3–8): Address the fastest-impact items first. Typically: crane productivity (often a gap in RTG/STS coordination), gate congestion (shift staggering or pre-advice enforcement), and storage invoicing accuracy (often billing disputes that slow cash flow). Phase 3 — Structural improvement (months 3–6): Tackle root causes — yard planning rules in TOS, equipment maintenance schedules, staff training, process redesign. Set new KPI targets with monthly review. Report to management monthly with trend charts showing improvement. Celebrate measurable wins to build team momentum.
