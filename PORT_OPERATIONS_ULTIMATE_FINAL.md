**Topic 2 — NOR (Notice of Readiness) — Critical Mastery**

Definition: NOR is the formal written notification from the Master of a vessel to the charterer, shipper, or their agent, stating that the vessel has arrived at the agreed destination and is ready in all respects to load or discharge cargo.

Keno critical: NOR tender er time theke laytime shuru hoy. Ek ghonta late NOR mane laytime ek ghonta late shuru — shipping line er financial consequence direct.

NOR Valid hobar conditions (all must be met simultaneously):

1. Vessel arrived at agreed port or berth
2. Vessel is at the agreed location (berth or anchorage as per charter)
3. Vessel is ready in all respects to commence operations
4. NOR tendered within agreed office hours (unless charter says otherwise)
5. Tendered to the correct party

NOR Disputes — Real Scenarios:

Scenario A: Vessel tenders NOR but hatch covers not removed. Terminal says "not ready in all respects." NOR invalid. Laytime does not start. Master disagrees. Dispute begins.

Scenario B: Vessel arrives Saturday. Charter says NOR only valid on business days. NOR tendered Saturday 10:00. Charterer says laytime starts Monday 08:00. Two days difference — thousands of dollars.

Scenario C: Vessel NOR tendered but customs not cleared. Terminal refuses to start — vessel "not arrived" in customs sense. NOR validity disputed.

Resolution: Charter party exact language controls. Port operations manager must know charter party terms before vessel arrives.

**Topic 3 — Laytime Calculation**

Definition: Laytime is the period of time agreed between parties during which the owner makes the vessel available for loading or discharging without payment beyond the freight. If laytime is exceeded, demurrage is incurred.

Core formula:

```
Laytime Allowed = agreed in charter party (e.g., 72 hours, or X TEU per day)
Laytime Used = actual time from NOR acceptance to completion of cargo ops
Demurrage = (Laytime Used minus Laytime Allowed) x Demurrage Rate per day
Despatch = (Laytime Allowed minus Laytime Used) x Despatch Rate per day (if vessel finishes early)
```

Real calculation example:

```
Vessel arrives: Monday 06:00
NOR tendered: Monday 06:30
Laytime commences: Monday 08:00 (office hours clause)
Laytime allowed: 36 hours
Cargo operations complete: Wednesday 02:00

Laytime used: Monday 08:00 to Wednesday 02:00 = 42 hours
Demurrage time: 42 minus 36 = 6 hours
Demurrage rate: USD 15,000 per day
Demurrage payable: 6/24 x 15,000 = USD 3,750
```

Exceptions that STOP laytime clock:

- Equipment breakdown if fault is on terminal side
- Bad weather if weather exception clause exists
- Sundays and holidays if SHINC (Sundays Holidays Included) not agreed
- Customs or immigration delay caused by port authority

### Day 3 and 4 — Tug and Pilot Operations

**Pilotage — Full Operational Detail**

Definition: Pilotage is the act of navigating a vessel through confined or dangerous waters by a licensed pilot who has specialist knowledge of local conditions.

Two types:

Compulsory pilotage: Law mandates a licensed pilot must board. Vessel master has no choice. Most commercial ports worldwide. Saudi Arabia — compulsory at all major ports.

Voluntary pilotage: Vessel master can choose. Less common. Some small ports.

Pilot boarding ground: The designated position outside the port where the pilot boards. Usually 3 to 10 nautical miles from berth.

SOLAS requirements for pilot boarding:

- Pilot ladder rigged correctly — manropes, lifebuoy ready
- Minimum freeboard: 9 meters — if more, combination ladder required
- Safe speed for boarding — not more than 6 knots
- Vessel to maintain VHF watch on agreed channel

Pilot's authority: Pilot advises Master. Master retains command legally. But practically pilot's navigational instructions are followed — because pilot knows the channel, currents, hazards.

Saudi pilot coordination:

At Jeddah Islamic Port — pilot boarding ground approximately 3 miles west of entrance channel. VTS monitors all movements. VHF Channel 16 for initial contact, then designated working channel.

**Tug Operations**

Bollard pull: The pulling force of a tug measured in tonnes. This determines what size vessel a tug can assist.

| Vessel Size | Minimum Tugs | Bollard Pull Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Feeder (below 3,000 TEU) | 1 to 2 tugs | 40 to 60 tonnes each |
| Medium (3,000 to 8,000 TEU) | 2 to 3 tugs | 60 to 80 tonnes each |
| Large (8,000 to 14,000 TEU) | 3 to 4 tugs | 80 to 100 tonnes each |
| Ultra Large (14,000+ TEU) | 4 to 6 tugs | 100+ tonnes each |

Tug attachment positions:

- Bow tug: Pushes or pulls the bow (front) of vessel
- Stern tug: Controls the rear during docking
- Breast tug: Alongside for lateral movement

Critical failure — tug line snap: During berthing, if tug line snaps in crosswind conditions, vessel can swing uncontrolled toward berth or other vessels. Emergency protocol: all tugs hold, pilot calls for additional tug, berthing aborted if necessary.

### Day 5 and 6 — Berth Allocation Strategy

**Berth Allocation — How Decisions Are Made**

Definition: Berth allocation is the systematic assignment of available berths to incoming vessels based on operational priorities, physical constraints, and commercial agreements.

Berth Allocation Priority Matrix (standard industry approach):

| Priority | Vessel Type / Situation | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Vessel with cargo on free time expiry | Financial penalty imminent |
| 2 | Mainline vessel (large, high revenue) | Revenue and schedule impact |
| 3 | Reefer vessel (time-sensitive cargo) | Cargo spoilage risk |
| 4 | Vessel in demurrage | Clock running, cost increasing |
| 5 | Feeder vessel | Lower revenue, more flexible |
| 6 | Vessel with dangerous goods (special berth) | Safety and compliance |
| 7 | Vessel on schedule | Standard rotation |

Physical berth constraints:

- Draft limit: Depth of water at berth versus vessel draft. Vessel cannot berth if draft exceeds depth.
- LOA limit: Length Overall of vessel versus berth length. Vessel cannot overhang neighboring vessel.
- SWL limit: Safe Working Load of berth versus vessel weight.
- Crane reach: Not all cranes can reach all vessel bays.
- Tide window: Some berths only accessible at high tide.

Saudi berth constraints:

Red Sea ports — relatively stable tide (0.5 to 1 meter range). Not major constraint. Gulf ports — slightly higher tide range. Dammam channel — draft restriction for large vessels — pilot tide window.

### Day 1 and 2 — Yard Operations Fundamentals

**Topic 1 — Bay-Row-Tier System (Container Address)**

Every container in a yard has an exact address. No different from a house address. Bay-Row-Tier is that address.

```
BAY: The longitudinal position — numbered forward to aft on vessel, or north to south in yard
ROW: The transverse position — left to right (Port to Starboard on vessel)
TIER: The vertical position — from bottom to top

Example address: BAY 042, ROW 06, TIER 04

Means: 42nd bay position, 6th row from port side, 4th container from bottom
```

Why critical: Crane operator cannot lift container without knowing exact address. TOS system assigns and tracks every address. Wrong address = wrong container lifted = cargo claim = vessel delay.

**Topic 2 — Yard Zoning**

| Zone | Contents | Special Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Import zone | Discharged containers awaiting delivery | Organized by shipping line and container size |
| Export zone | Containers received, awaiting vessel load | Organized by vessel, bay plan sequence |
| Transshipment zone | Discharged, to be loaded on connecting vessel | Minimum dwell — maximum 3 days target |
| Reefer zone | Temperature-controlled containers | Power plugs, 24/7 monitoring, temp logging |
| OOG zone | Out of Gauge — oversize containers | Special flat racks, open tops, separate area |
| Hazardous zone | IMDG classified dangerous goods | Segregation by class, minimum distance from other cargo |
| Empty zone | Empty containers | Separate area, stackable higher |
| Customs hold zone | Containers under customs inspection | No movement without customs release |

Saudi specific: Reefer zone at Jeddah — extreme heat management. Ambient temperature up to 48 degrees Celsius in summer. Reefer units must work harder. Power supply reliability critical. Monitoring every 2 hours minimum.

**Topic 3 — Dwell Time — The Number That Controls Everything**

Definition: Dwell time is the number of days a container remains within the terminal from the moment of discharge (import) or gate-in (export) until the moment it exits the terminal.

Why it matters:

- High dwell = yard fills up = no space for new discharge = cranes stop = vessel delay
- High dwell = more storage revenue but worse operation
- High dwell import = importer paying storage = delays = complaints
- Low dwell = good flow = better yard utilization = more throughput capacity

Industry targets:

| Container Type | Target Dwell | Saudi Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Import | Below 5 days | 7 to 12 days average |
| Transshipment | Below 3 days | 4 to 7 days |
| Export | Below 3 days from gate-in | Generally met |
| Hazardous | Below 2 days | Varies |

Dwell escalation triggers: 5-day notice to agent, 10-day formal letter, 14-day customs notification, 21-day auction process begins (country dependent).

### Day 3 and 4 — Crane Systems

**STS — Ship-to-Shore Crane**

Definition: A large gantry crane positioned on the quay that spans the vessel beam and lifts containers between the vessel and quayside.

Key specifications:

- Outreach: Distance crane arm extends over vessel — typically 55 to 70 meters (for ultra large vessels)
- SWL: Safe Working Load — typically 65 to 100 tonnes
- Lift height: Above rail — typically 40 to 50 meters
- Hoist speed: 75 to 120 meters per minute (speed = productivity)
- Trolley speed: 180 to 240 meters per minute

Productivity measurement:

```
Gross Crane Rate (GCR): Total moves in shift / Total shift hours
(includes all time — breakdown, waiting, shift change, everything)
Target: 25 to 35 moves per hour

Net Crane Rate (NCR): Total moves / Active lifting time only
(excludes non-productive time)
Target: 30 to 40 moves per hour

Berth Productivity: Total moves on vessel / Total vessel time at berth
This is what shipping lines care about most
```

**RTG — Rubber Tyre Gantry Crane**

Definition: A yard crane that straddles a stack of containers and moves on rubber tyres — can drive between stacks.

Key facts:

- Stacks up to 5 containers high (5 over 5 configuration common)
- Row width: 6 to 8 containers per row under one RTG
- Moves between stacks on rubber tyres — flexible, no fixed track
- Powered by diesel generator or electric (cable reel)
- Speed: 70 to 130 meters per minute travel
- Each RTG typically handles 1 to 2 prime movers simultaneously

**RMG — Rail Mounted Gantry**

Definition: A yard crane that runs on fixed rails — cannot move between stacks but more precise and faster within its lane.

- Used in automated terminals — King Abdullah Port uses RMGs
- Higher throughput per crane than RTG
- Less flexible — fixed to their rail lane
- Automated versions run without human operator

**Equipment Chain — How It All Connects**

```
STS crane discharges container from vessel
        ↓
Container placed on prime mover (terminal truck) on quayside
        ↓
Prime mover drives container to assigned yard block
        ↓
RTG or RMG lifts container from prime mover
        ↓
RTG places container at assigned Bay-Row-Tier address in yard
        ↓
TOS records new position — CODECO sent
```

Synchronization problem: If prime movers are fewer than crane can handle — STS crane waits. Crane rate drops. Vessel delayed. This is the most common productivity bottleneck.

### Day 5 to 8 — Gate Operations, Failure Cases, KPIs

**Gate Operations**

Definition: Gate is the terminal's physical and administrative control point where trucks enter and exit the terminal with containers.

Standard truck gate process:

```
Step 1: Truck arrives at outer gate
OCR cameras read license plate, container number automatically
Step 2: System checks — is truck appointment valid? Is container released by customs?
Step 3: If all checks pass — gate opens, truck proceeds
Step 4: Truck goes to assigned RTG block
Step 5: RTG loads/unloads container on truck
Step 6: Truck exits through out-gate — weight check, container number verified
Step 7: CODECO message sent — container departed terminal
```

Truck TAT (Turnaround Time): Gate-in to gate-out. Industry target below 45 minutes. Saudi average 60 to 90 minutes due to documentation checks.

Congestion management:

- Truck appointment system: Trucks book time slots — spread volume
- Gate split: Multiple entry lanes — import lane, export lane, empty lane
- Appointment throttling: System limits bookings per hour
- Off-peak incentive: Cheaper rates for night and weekend appointments

**Phase 03 Key Failure Cases**

Failure Case 01 — Yard Saturation:

Yard above 90% utilization. Cranes cannot find empty space to place containers. RTG movements increase — rehandle ratio spikes. Every container needs to be moved before the target container can be accessed. Productivity collapses.

Fix: Emergency measures — suspend gate-in for 6 to 12 hours, contact shipping lines to accelerate delivery, notify customs for expedited release, identify low-risk containers for temporary offsite storage.

Failure Case 02 — Rehandle Cascade:

Export containers not pre-marshalled by bay sequence. When vessel arrives and crane starts loading — every second container requires the RTG to move 3 to 4 other containers first. What should be 30 moves per hour becomes 15 moves per hour. Vessel delivery performance halved.

Fix: Pre-marshalling window — 24 to 48 hours before vessel arrival, yard controllers reorganize export zone by vessel bay sequence.

**Phase 03 KPIs**

| KPI | Definition | Target | Impact If Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gross crane rate | Total moves / total shift hours | 25 to 35 MPH | Vessel TAT increases |
| Net crane rate | Moves / active time | 30 to 40 MPH | Shipping line SLA breach |
| Yard utilization | Used slots / total slots | Below 85% | Above 90% — operations degrade |
| Rehandle ratio | Non-productive moves / total moves | Below 8% | Crane rate drops, cost increases |
| Truck TAT | Gate-in to gate-out | Below 45 minutes | Congestion, driver complaints |
| Equipment availability | Cranes operational / total cranes | Above 90% | Reduced crane deployment |

### Day 9 — Saudi Infrastructure Context

**King Abdullah Port (KAP) — Automation Model**

Operator: Saudi Global Ports (DP World + Saudi partners)

Automation level:

- Automated gate — full OCR, no human at primary gate
- Automated RTGs (ARTGs) in yard — remote operated, GPS guided
- TOS: NAVIS N4 — fully integrated
- FASAH direct integration — real-time cargo release
- RFID on all containers and trucks
- Drone-based yard inspection (pilot program)

Capacity: 2.5 million TEU per year, expanding to 5 million TEU by 2030

Why KAP matters for your career: KAP needs supervisors who understand both the automated system and can manage when automation fails. Human override knowledge is critical. An experienced operator like you — with real yard understanding — is valuable even in an automated terminal.

**Reefer Operations in Saudi Heat**

Challenge: Ambient temperature 45 to 48 degrees in summer. Reefer units set to minus 18 (frozen) or plus 2 to 8 (chilled). Unit works 3 to 4 times harder than in temperate climate. Power consumption doubles. Breakdown risk triples.

Operational requirements:

- Check every reefer every 2 hours minimum
- Pre-cooling of plug points before vessel arrival
- Backup generator for reefer zone — power interruption is cargo loss
- Calibrated temperature log — MARPOL requirement for some cargo types
- Reporting to shipping line every 4 hours for sensitive cargo
